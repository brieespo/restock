How To Use

Staples & variants

Every need you track is a staple — "Face wash," "Paper towels" — and each brand/size you actually buy for it is a variant underneath. Track just one brand and it looks and behaves exactly like a plain product. Tap ＋ Track another brand for this to add a second variant — that's when the staple gets its own name field and a brand comparison table (unit price, rating, average time between purchases) appears so you can see which one's actually winning.

Four statuses, one tap

Status lives on the staple, not any one brand: Stocked → Running low → Out → Ordered → back to Stocked. Standing in the bathroom noticing it's almost gone? One tap and future-you remembers.

Category tabs & search

Tabs split the catalog by category; search covers staple names, every variant's brand and notes, and stores. The Needs attention filter shows everything that's low, out, or on order.

Paste a list

The fastest way in: More → Paste a list, one product per line as "Brand – Product – Store" (a plain product name works too). Each line becomes its own staple with one variant. Import, then fix details on any card whenever.

On-hand & Finished one

Each variant can track how many you have on hand. Tapping Bought it! adds to it (quantity is editable, defaults to 1); tapping Finished one subtracts one and logs the date. The staple card shows the total on hand across every variant.

Discontinued watch

Flag a specific variant when it's being phased out. Flagged variants get their own standing Discontinued watch panel on Home — not buried in the catalog — so "stock up if you see it" stays visible until you tap Bought it! or Stop watching.

Your rebuying profile

The italic line at the top of Home reads your own data back to you — which brands you're loyal to, how many products you rebuy a month, and which store wins on price most often. It's rule-based (no guessing), sharpens as you log more purchases, and stays quiet until there's real data behind it.

Bought it! & the rebuy prediction

Tap Bought it! any time you restock something — if a staple has more than one variant it'll ask which (defaulting to whichever you bought most recently), then logs today's date and quantity (both editable), prefills the last price/store, and marks the staple Stocked. Once a staple has 2+ logged purchases pooled across its variants, Restock learns your real rebuy pace and predicts when you'll need it again. With just one purchase, set a rough "rebuy every ~N days" estimate on the edit screen until the real pattern emerges.

Predicted due soon — now stock-aware

Once you're logging on-hand quantities and the occasional Finished one, the prediction upgrades: it multiplies your usage rate (median days between finishes) by how many units you have left, so a staple with backups shows "covered until ~date" and only surfaces as it actually nears zero. Without enough on-hand/finish data yet, it falls back to the plain rebuy-pace date — "you usually rebuy around now." Either way it's a signal separate from status. Tap Bought it! if it's time, or Still fine, +2wk to snooze it — snoozing gently widens the prediction so the radar gets quieter and more accurate the more you use it. Marking something Out earlier than predicted does the opposite, tightening the estimate.

Shopping mode

The Shopping tab groups everything you currently need — running low, out, on order, or due soon — by store, checklist style, with an estimated total per store. Check items off as you grab them, then tap Mark checked as bought to log all of them as purchased at once (today's date, that store, listed price) — the fastest way to feed the interval engine.

Restock rhythm

The heat-strip at the top of Shopping mode previews predicted run-outs over the next 8 weeks — a quiet week or two, then a cluster lighting up together, is your "big Costco run" signal. Tap a week to see exactly what's predicted to run out then.

Price watch & shrinkflation

Add a size when logging a purchase (e.g. "19 oz") and Restock tracks price-per-unit over time for that variant. Once the same size has been bought at meaningfully different times, a noticeable jump (8%+) surfaces as a Price watch panel on Home and a chip on the card — the kind of creeping cost nobody notices without the data.
